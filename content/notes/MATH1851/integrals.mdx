## Definite integrals


<Block variant="primary" title="Signed areas">
Signed area is the area between the curve and the x-axis, where the area above the x-axis is positive and below is negative.
</Block>


<Block variant="knowledge" title="Properties of definite intergrals">

    1. $\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx=-\int_{b}^{a}f(x)dx$
    2. $\int_{a}^{a}f(x)dx=0$
    3. $\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx+\int_{b}^{c}f(x)dx=\int_{a}^{c}f(x)dx$
    4. $\int_{a}^{b}kf(x)dx=k\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx$
    5. $\int_{a}^{b}(f(x)\pm g(x))dx=\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx\pm\int_{a}^{b}g(x)dx$
</Block>


## Fundamental theorem of calculus


<Block variant="secondary" title="Fundamental theorem of calculus (FTC)">
If $f(x)$ is continuous in $[a,b]$, then:
$\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx=F(b)-F(a)$
Where $F(x)$ is the definite integral of $f(x)$.
</Block>



<Block variant="secondary" title="Second fundamental theorem of calculus">
If $f(x)$ is continuous in $[a,b]$, then:
$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{a}^{x}f(t)dt=f(x)$
<BlockSep />
Find $g'(x)$ for $g(x)=\int_1^{x^2}\cos t\ dt$:
We first define $G(u)=\int_1^{u}\cos t\ dt$, then we can apply the chain rule:
```math
g'(x) & =(G(x^2))' \&=G'(x^2)\cdot2x\&=\cos x^2\cdot2x
```
</Block>


<Block variant="knowledge" title="Cases of area 0">
For $\int^{b}_{a}f(x)dx$:

    - If $b=a$, then the area is 0 (no width)
    - If $f(x)=f(-x)$ and $a=-b$, then the area is 0 (symmetry)
</Block>


<Block variant="secondary" title="Limit of Riemann sums">
We can convert a limit to infinity of a Riemann sum to a definite integral:
$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(a+i\Delta x)\Delta x=\int_{0}^{b}f(a+x)dx,    \Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$
Some properties of summation:

    1. $x\sum f(x) = \sum x f(x)$
    2. $\sum_{i=1+n} f(x, i), \text{let }k=i-n\to\sum_{k=1}^{n}f(x, k+n)$
</Block>




## Techniques of integration


<Block variant="knowledge" title="Overview">

    - By [**substitution**](#sec-intsub): “sub $g(x)\to u$, find $du$ and replace all functions of $x(dx)$ with $u(du)$”
    - By [**part**](#sec-intpart): “$\int ab=a\int b-\int (a'\times \int b)$”
    - By **joining recurring parts**: “$\int ab=f(ab)+\int ab\to 2\int ab = f(ab)$”
    - By **partial fractions**: “$\frac{k}{f(x)g(x)}=\frac{A}{f(x)}+\frac{B}{g(x)}$”
    - By [**trigonometric identities**](#sec-inttrigo)
    - By **multiplying fractions by one**: “Multiplying $\frac{\sec^2x}{\sec^2x}$ to fit trigo form”
</Block>




<Block variant="secondary" title="Integration by substitution">
For $\int f(g)g'\:dx$:

    1. Let $u=g$
    2. Find $du=g'dx$
    3. Change limits in terms of $u$ if definite
    4. Replace all $g\to u$ and $g'dx\to du$
    5. Integrate, then subsitute $u\to g$
</Block>




<Block variant="secondary" title="Integration by parts">
$\int ab=a\int b-\int (a'\times \int b)$
*Tips:*
Let $a$ (differentiating term) to the first term you see on the list:

    1. **L**ogarithmic
    2. **I**nverse trigo
    3. **A**lebratic (polynomial)
    4. **T**rigo
    5. **E**xponential
</Block>


<Block variant="knowledge" title="Deriving the IBP formula">
The formula is derived from the product rule of differentiation:
```math
(ab)'    & = a'b + b'a                     \\
b'a      & = (ab)' - a'b                   \\
\int b'a & = ab - \int a'b                 \\
\int ab  & =a\int b-\int (a'\times \int b)
```
</Block>




<Block variant="knowledge" title="Trigo-identities substitution tips">
Use the following trigonometric identities to simplify the integral:

    - $\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1$
    - $\sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos2x}{2}$
    - $\cos^2x=\frac{1+\cos2x}{2}$
    - $2\sin x\cos x=\sin2x$
    - $\cos2x=\cos^2x-\sin^2x$
    - $\sec^2x=1+\tan^2x$
    - $\csc^2x=1+\cot^2x$
Use the following substitutions for similar expressions:
$
\text{Expressions of the form }
a^2-f(x)^2 & \to f(x)=a\sin\theta \\
a^2+f(x)^2 & \to f(x)=a\tan\theta \\
f(x)^2-a^2 & \to f(x)=a\sec\theta \\
$
Remember that this uses *integration by substitution*, so we need to find $dx$ in terms of $\theta$.
</Block>


## Solids of revolution




<Block variant="primary" title="Solids of revolution">
A 3D shape formed by rotating a *region* around an axis.
<BlockSep />
A region is defined by 2 curves and an interval:

    1. The vertical region bounded by an outer cruve $R(x)$ and an inner curve $r(x)$.
    2. The inner curve is $y=0$ for the region bounded by the $x$-axis.
    3. The horizontal region is bounded by an interval $x:\ [a, b]$
Note that for the methods below, the terms $x$ and $y$ can be *switched* to fit the problem.
$\{f(x)\to\ \circlearrowright x\}$ denotes using a function of $x$ to find the volume of a solid of revolution around the $x$-axis.

    1. If the volume is generated by the rotation about an axis other than the main axes, we can simply *shift* the function to fit the main axes.
    2. If the curves in the given range does not form a closed region with only acute angles, we can *split* the region into multiple parts.
</Block>


<Block variant="secondary" title="Volume by Washers (Disk)">
$\{f(x)\to\ \circlearrowright x\}:     V=\pi\int_{a}^{b}R^2(x)-r^2(x)dx$
For rotating about $y=n$: $\forall R/r(x)\to n-R/r(x)$
</Block>


<Block variant="secondary" title="Volume by Cylindrical shells">
$\{f(x)\to\ \circlearrowright y\}:     V=2\pi\int_{a}^{b}[x][R(x)-r(x)]dx$
For rotating about $x=n$: $[x]\to [x-n]$
</Block>



## Arcs and surfaces


<Block variant="secondary" title="Arc length">
The *arc length* of a curve $y=f(x)$ from $x=a$ to $x=b$ is given by:
$L=\int_{a}^{b}\sqrt{1+f'^2(x)}dx$
And for parametized equations $x=f(t)$, $y=g(t)$:
$L=\int_{a}^{b}\sqrt{f'^2(t)+g'^2(t)}dt$
For polar equations $r=f(\theta)$:
$L=\int_{a}^{b}\sqrt{r^2+r'^2(\theta)}d\theta$
</Block>


<Block variant="secondary" title="Area surfaces of revolution">
Used to find the *surface area* generated by rotating a curve along an axis.
$\{f(x)\to\ \circlearrowright x\}:     A=2\pi\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\sqrt{1+f'^2(x)}dx$
And for parametized equations $x=f(t)$, $y=g(t)$:
$A=2\pi\int_{a}^{b}g(t)\sqrt{f'^2(t)+g'^2(t)}dt$
</Block>
